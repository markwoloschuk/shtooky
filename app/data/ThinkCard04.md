[paragraph]
WYSIWYG. What You See Is What You Get. Not just the funnest acronym ever conceived, it was a revolution in desktop publishing. Finally, the screen showed you what the printer would produce. No more gap between intent and output. No more surprises.

[pullquote]
Ceci n’est pas un pipe

[paragraph]
Anyone that’s wrestled with color management software recognizes this is as more of an aspiration than achieved reality. And there’s a deeper philosophical conflict when substituting pixels for reality. Rene Magritte’s famous image of a pipe that is, of course, not actually a pipe is one of my favorite examples. But in creative work I think there’s a broader lesson as well.

[pullquote]
<Perspective> is more
important than perception

[paragraph]
What you see is never all there is. It's a function of where you're standing, what angle you're at, what you already believe, what you're primed to notice. Two people look at the same brief and see different problems. They look at the same cut and feel different things. Some see a finished piece and others see what's missing.

[paragraph]
This lesson really sank in when I was shooting documentaries. You commit to a shot from one vantage point — get what you need — and then you move. Not because the first angle was wrong – because the second angle shows you something the first one couldn't.

[pullquote]
Commit and move on

[paragraph]
I’ll do this any time I take photos. My friends tease me for getting on the ground in odd places:  airports, parks, wedding venues – anywhere there's something worth seeing from a different height. I can’t help it. The view from the ground is different than from above.

[paragraph]
The full picture requires more than one vantage point – sometimes more than one pair of eyes. Collaboration is perspective access — other people occupy angles you can't reach alone. The best creative partnerships I've had weren't with people who saw what I saw, they were with people who saw what I missed.

[paragraph]
I built a slightly ridiculous interactive art piece called “Make-Out in the Third Person.” I was playing with this idea of how a different perspective changes your experience. Participants wore FPV video goggles that were being fed an image coming from a camera on the end of a long boom pole. Instead of seeing through their own eyes, they saw themselves from several feet away, almost as if they were characters in someone else's film. Then I invited them to kiss. This new and unique point of view changed their relationship to the moment and themselves. 

[paragraph]
There’s a real power in seeing things from a different perspective. So stand somewhere else occasionally.
