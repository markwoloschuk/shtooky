[paragraph]
WYSIWYG — What You See Is What You Get — was a promise made by early software: the screen will show you exactly what will print. It was a revolutionary idea. It was also, as a principle applied to design feedback, completely wrong. Two people looking at the same design see different things. They always have. What you see is not what I get. What the client sees is not what the audience will see. The acronym lied.

[paragraph]
Experience shapes perception. A designer looking at a page sees structure, hierarchy, rhythm, tension. A client looks at the same page and sees what their competitor did last quarter. An end user sees whether it answers their question in the next three seconds or doesn't. None of these views are wrong. They're all partial. The design is the overlap — and the designer's job, before anything else, is to understand the full shape of what everyone is actually looking at.

[pullquote]
The gap between what you see[br]
and what they see is where design <lives>.

[paragraph]
I once presented a piece of work that I was genuinely proud of. The client said it felt "cold." I'd read the brief as sophisticated and contemporary. They'd lived with their brand long enough that those words meant something entirely different to them than to me. We weren't looking at the same thing. Neither of us was wrong. But only one of us had done the job of anticipating the other's view — and it wasn't me that day.

[paragraph]
Naming this gap is half the design job. When you can articulate why your stakeholder sees something differently — not just "they don't get it," but specifically what they're bringing to the room — you can actually design for the distance between you. That's the skill. Not getting everyone to see the same thing. Getting close enough to the other person's vantage point that the work makes sense from where they're standing.
