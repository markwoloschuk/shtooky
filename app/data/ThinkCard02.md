[paragraph]
Design is often described as problem-solving, which makes it sound like an engineering task. You identify a problem. You engineer a solution. Clean, logical, done. But the best design work I’ve done — and the best I’ve seen from anyone else — doesn’t start with a problem. It starts with a feeling. Not the designer’s feeling. Someone else’s. I helped produce and shoot a documentary about the technological Singularity. I went in expecting to become more interested in technology. I came out more interested in people.

[pullquote]
Myself included

[paragraph]
People often think great documentaries begin with extraordinary stories. My experience was almost the opposite. Almost any story can become compelling if you understand it deeply enough. That means uncovering not just the facts, but the emotional conflict beneath them. To do that requires empathy—for the subject, and for the audience.

[paragraph]
A good documentary becomes a window of empathy. It lets us step inside someone else’s experience for a while. But it also reflects something back at us. We don’t just learn about the subject—we learn about ourselves.
Working with filmmaker Doug Wolens on “the Singularity” we explored the more metaphysical aspects of technology.  I came to understand my affinity for it was rooted in wanting greater connection with people and to know myself better.

[pullquote]
Design with heart

[paragraph]
I think the same idea applies in design. Empathy isn’t a step in the process — a box you check before you get to the real work – it’s the lens through which the whole process runs. You need to feel the client’s problem before you try to solve it. Feel the audience’s need before you try to meet it. Feel your collaborator’s perspective before you try to change it.

[pullquote]
Empathy in,
emotion out

[praragraph]
The empathy lens is what helps you find the emotional hooks — where they live in the material, where they can be placed, how hard to pull. I point it in three directions. Toward the subject — what is this really about? Toward the audience — what do they actually need to feel? And toward myself — am I bringing my full attention, or am I on autopilot?

[paragraph]
Any story can be great if you empathize deeply enough with the people in it. Any brief can be cracked if you empathize deeply enough with the person who wrote it.
