[paragraph]
Design is often described as problem-solving, which makes it sound like an engineering task. You identify a problem. You engineer a solution. Clean, logical, done. But the best design work I've done — and the best I've seen from anyone else — doesn't start with a problem. It starts with a feeling. Not the designer's feeling. Someone else's.

[paragraph]
The empathy lens is what helps you see the emotional hooks and where they can be placed. It's not a research methodology. It's a disposition — an active practice of imagining yourself into another person's experience with enough specificity that you start to understand not just what they need but what they feel. What embarrasses them. What delights them. What they'd never say out loud but would recognize instantly when you get it right.

[pullquote]
Design that doesn't know[br]
how it <feels> is just furniture.

[paragraph]
I worked on a campaign for a product that people were ashamed to admit they needed. The brief called for aspirational, optimistic messaging — the standard playbook. But when I actually spent time with the audience, what I felt in every conversation was relief. Not pride. Relief. So we built the whole thing around that. The shame dissolved. The campaign connected. It wasn't a smarter strategy — it was a more honest one.

[paragraph]
Empathy doesn't make you soft. It makes you accurate. It sharpens your eye for what actually matters in a given piece of work and dulls your attachment to things that only matter to you. Every choice that lands — every image that makes someone stop, every line that makes someone lean in — lands because it understood something true about the person receiving it. That understanding doesn't come from strategy decks. It comes from the willingness to see with your heart first.
