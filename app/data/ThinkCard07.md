---
title: Iterate but don't thrash
narrowtitle: Iterate but[br]don't thrash
subtitle: Patience is a creative skill, so is knowing when to move on.
imagePath: /images/think/07/
---

[paragraph]
When I was working for Facebook’s comms team I once did thirty-two rounds on an infographic. Thirty-two! 

[paragraph]
I'm not complaining — the client was engaged, the feedback was real, and round thirty-two was genuinely better than round one. But somewhere around round twenty-five I had a quiet realization: we were past the point of meaningful improvement and onto a hamster wheel of preference cycling. A change would come back. Then the previous version would come back. Then a hybrid. Then we'd land somewhere close to where we'd been three rounds ago and call it done.

[pullquote]
Somebody was paying
for all of this

[paragraph]
Iteration is how good work becomes great work. The discipline is knowing which kind of iteration you're doing. Productive iteration has direction. Each round answers a question, resolves a tension, moves the work closer to what it needs to be. You can feel it — the thing getting sharper, the argument getting clearer, the composition settling into itself.

[paragraph]
Thrashing doesn't have direction. It has energy — sometimes a lot of it — but it's circular. You're not refining, you're restless. The work isn't getting better, you're just not done moving it around. Patience is the skill that keeps you iterating when you think you're already there — because sometimes round twenty-eight proves you wrong. Knowing when to move on is the skill that stops you cycling when the work is done and you just haven't admitted it yet.

[pullquote]
Check one with the other

[paragraph]
Patience and restraint aren't opposing forces. They're partners. Patience asks, "Have we explored this enough?" Restraint asks, "Are we still learning anything?"

[paragraph]
Good creative judgment comes from listening to both.
