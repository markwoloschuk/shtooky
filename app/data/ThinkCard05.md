---
title: Design is a conversation
narrowtitle: Design is[br]a conversation
subtitle: The brief starts the conversation, it doesn't end it.
imagePath: /images/think/05/
---


[paragraph]
Good design is never a monologue. It's always a response to something — to a brief, to a constraint, to the audience's actual behavior, to what turned out to be true after the first round that wasn't obvious before. The most useful thing you can do with a brief is start a conversation about it. Push back where it's vague. Question the assumptions underneath the objectives. Not to be difficult — to make sure the problem you're solving is actually the problem.




[paragraph]
Questions are the greatest design tools – fast, cheap and everyone knows how to use them.


[paragraph]
A question I often ask on a video project is: why is this a video? Not to be difficult. Because it's the most important question. A video is a choice — expensive, time-consuming, a specific kind of experience for a specific kind of attention. If the answer to "why is this a video" is "because we always do videos" or "because the brief said video," that's worth knowing before you shoot a single frame.

[paragraph]
Sometimes the answer is obvious and good and the video proceeds. Sometimes the question opens a door.

[paragraph]
The brief is not a set of instructions. It's an opening statement. It says: here is what we think we need, here is what we know, here is what worries us. A good brief is generous — it gives you enough context to think, not enough answers to stop thinking. The designers who treat it as the final word on what the work should be end up making work that answers questions no one is still asking.

[paragraph]
Design doesn't start in a box that you escape through creativity. It starts in formless infinity — endless possibility, no direction, no constraints. The brief is how you build the box. Questions, definitions, constraints, decisions — each one a wall that makes the space more workable. A good brief isn't a limitation. It's the architecture that makes good decisions possible.

[paragraph]
And good design, once it's in the world, keeps the conversation going. The best work leaves something unsaid — not because it's incomplete, but because the gap is where the audience enters. I once designed a t-shirt with a bold graphic and no text. People kept asking what it meant. That was the point. The question was the whole idea.

[paragraph]
Good design is never a monologue. In the making, it asks questions. In the world, it starts them.

[paragraph]
So ask. Early, often, and without apology. The brief is the opening line. Everything that follows is the conversation.
