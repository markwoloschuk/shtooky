title: Design is a conversation
narrowtitle: Design is[br]a conversation
subtitle: The brief starts the conversation, it doesn't end it.
imagePath: /images/think/05/
---

[paragraph]
The brief is not a set of instructions. It's an opening statement. It says: here is what we think we need, here is what we know, here is what worries us. A good brief is generous — it gives you enough context to think, not enough answers to stop thinking. The designers who treat it as the final word on what the work should be end up making work that answers questions no one is still asking.

[paragraph]
Good design is never a monologue. It's always a response to something — to a brief, to a constraint, to the audience's actual behavior, to what turned out to be true after the first round that wasn't obvious before. The most useful thing you can do with a brief is start a conversation about it. Push back where it's vague. Question the assumptions underneath the objectives. Not to be difficult — to make sure the problem you're solving is actually the problem.

[pullquote]
The best work happens[br]
when the brief <changes>.

[paragraph]
I had a brief once that asked for something that would reach young adults. What it actually needed — and what only became clear after three rounds of conversation with the client — was something that would reach parents who still thought of themselves as young adults. Same demographic label, completely different emotional register. The brief didn't know that. The process of talking to the people who wrote it eventually surfaced it.

[paragraph]
This is why process matters beyond efficiency. Not because it produces a paper trail. Because conversation is how you discover what the problem actually is. The brief gives you the starting conditions. The work you do around it — the questions, the presentations, the reactions, the pivots — is the design itself. The artefacts are just what that conversation eventually settles into.
